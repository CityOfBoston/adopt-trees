The Urban Forest

Increasing Boston's urban forest will benefit city residents through environmental and aesthetic improvements. City trees enrich the lives of all residents by providing many benefits, including: beautification of city streets, providing a respite on hot summer days, improved air and water quality, and offsetting our carbon emissions.

Greenovate Boston and the Grow Boston Greener campaign aim to plant 100,000 trees by 2020 with the overarching goal of increasing Boston's tree canopy to 35% by 2030. The city is planting over 1,000 trees a year to meet this goal!

Newly planted trees have a difficult time getting established in a tough urban environment. Often these young trees die within a few seasons and new trees are planted in their place. Although young trees do provide some benefits, it is the mature trees that will increase the overall tree canopy and provide positive environmental impacts.

In order to help the young street trees reach a mature state, the City needs help from individuals, businesses, and organizations. Continue reading to learn how you can help Boston's urban forest grow!

Tree Care Tips

By helping to water and care for a young street tree, you can help build a healthy tree canopy on your street. Some quick tips are listed below, but be sure to download the tree care guide for more detailed advice.

Water Weekly
Newly planted trees should be watered from May to October. They need 10 gallons once a week and twice as much in hot months like July and August.

Tend to the Tree Pit
Three main tips:

  1. Tree pits need to be cleared of trash.
  2. The top three inches of soil should be loosened.
  3. Mulch will help keep the tree roots moist.

Protect and Beautify
Protect: Placing a protective barrier around the tree pit will help to contain mulch and keep people and pets from walking across the tree pit.

Beautify: Planting small flowers in the tree pit is good for the tree and further beautifies the neighborhood.

Helpful Tools
Some helpful tools include: a shovel, soil cultivator, gardening gloves, hose or watering can (THIS WATER CAN carries 6 gallons and has a spout to pour in a treegator).

Workshops
Tree care workshops will be offered in the spring and fall through the Boston Natural Areas Network.
